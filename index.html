Every physical prediction — weather, jet engines, drug binding, brain imaging — comes from solving a PDE.

FEM turns the PDE into a sparse linear (or non-linear) system on a mesh. The finer the mesh, the more accurate — and the more expensive.

10⁶–10⁷ unknowns at production resolution
Every PDE solve is the bottleneck behind a real-world prediction.

Camp 1 — Classical solvers (FOM)

The approach

Finite Element Method + Conjugate Gradient

\[ K u = F \]

Discretise the PDE into a giant sparse system. Solve directly with an iterative Krylov method (CG for SPD problems).

Trade-offs

What you get / what it costs

  • Provably accurate (convergence theory)
  • Conservation laws preserved
  • Boundary conditions enforced exactly
  • Sparse matrix-vector multiplies are memory-bound on GPUs
  • Iteration count grows with mesh refinement
  • Cost scales as $\mathcal{O}(I_{\text{Krylov}} \cdot N_z)$
~100 seconds per CG solve at $N{=}10^6$ on a single A100
The gold standard for accuracy. The bottleneck for everything that needs many solves.

Camp 2 — Neural surrogates

The approach

FNO · DeepONet · PINN

\[ u \approx \mathrm{NN}(\text{input};\, \theta) \]

Train a neural network to learn the solution operator end-to-end. At inference, one forward pass replaces the entire PDE solve.

Trade-offs

What you get / what it costs

  • Fast inference (~10× over CG)
  • One trained model handles many parameter inputs
  • One trained model = one fixed (accuracy, speed) point
  • No conservation guarantees
  • BCs only via soft penalty in the loss
  • Stiff systems & advection-dominated cases often fail
No knob to dial — want better accuracy? Train another model.
Fast — but a single trained model gives one fixed accuracy/speed point. No tuning at deployment.

The middle path — Non-linear Manifold ROM

Use a neural net to compress.  Use Galerkin to solve.
Property Classical FOM Neural surrogate NM-ROM
Provably accurate ✓ ⚠ ✓
Fast inference ✗ ✓ ✓ (up to 191×)
BCs enforced exactly ✓ ✗ ✓ (via masking)
Tunable at inference ⚠ (just iters) ✗ ✓ (3 knobs, no retraining)
The neural net only does the compression — turning a million unknowns into ~24 latent numbers. The actual PDE is still solved with classical numerics (Galerkin projection + Gauss-Newton), just in the small space. That's how we keep the accuracy guarantees of FOM while getting the speed of ML.

Part 2

The Method

Step by step, with the math made visual

Roadmap — four ideas, in order

1

Compress

Autoencoder squashes the FEM state from $N$ down to $k$ latent numbers. Mask enforces Dirichlet BCs exactly.

2

Project

Galerkin projection: left-multiply the FEM residual by $J_\mathcal{D}^\top$ to collapse $N$ equations into $k$.

3

Solve

Gauss-Newton in the $k$-dim latent space. For Heat: backward-Euler in latent space.

4

Speed up

Empirical Quadrature picks $\sim 1\%$ of nodes. JAX gives $J\!\cdot\!v$ matrix-free.

1. Non-linear Manifold & Boundary Enforcement

Encode The encoder compresses the FEM state from $N$ DoFs down to $k$ latent coords: \[ z = \mathcal{E}(u), \quad u \in \mathbb{R}^N,\ z \in \mathbb{R}^k,\ k \ll N \]

Decode The decoder reconstructs the field from the latent code: \[ \hat{u} = \mathcal{D}(z) \]

Mask & lift A binary mask $m$ kills boundary nodes, then a lifting vector $u_g$ pastes the Dirichlet values back in: \[ \tilde{u}(z)\;=\;m \odot \mathcal{D}(z)\;+\;u_g \] Why? Because $m_i = 0$ on $\Gamma$ forces $\partial \tilde{u}/\partial z = 0$ on the boundary — so during Galerkin projection, every boundary equation is annihilated automatically. No penalty terms, no soft constraints.

Why project? — the geometric picture

After Sundar (2026), Fig. 2 — latent Gauss-Newton step.

The one-line idea: we can only correct in directions we can move. The decoder traps us on a $k$-dim surface inside $\mathbb{R}^N$. The residual $R$ points anywhere — but we move only along the manifold. Galerkin = keep the component of $R$ that lies in the tangent plane.
Galerkin orthogonality: \[ J_\mathcal{D}^\top R(\tilde{u}) = 0 \] Columns of $J_\mathcal{D}$ span the tangent plane. $J_\mathcal{D}^\top R$ takes the inner product with each tangent direction. Setting it to zero $\Rightarrow$ residual is purely normal to $\mathcal{M}$ — the best we can do without leaving the manifold.

2. Galerkin Projection — Poisson (steady)

Residual FEM discretisation of $-\Delta u = f$ gives the linear system \[ R(u) = Ku - F \;=\; 0, \qquad K \in \mathbb{R}^{N\times N} \]

Substitute Plug the masked manifold $\tilde{u}(z)$ into the residual: \[ R(\tilde{u}(z)) = K\tilde{u}(z) - F \;\in\; \mathbb{R}^N \]

Project Left-multiply by $J_\mathcal{D}^\top$ — the residual collapses from $\mathbb{R}^N$ to $\mathbb{R}^k$: \[ r_\text{red}(z) \;=\; J_\mathcal{D}^\top(z)\bigl(K\tilde{u}(z) - F\bigr) \;=\; 0 \]

Gauss-Newton Solve the $k \times k$ system: \[ \Delta z \;=\; -\bigl(J_\mathcal{D}^\top K\, J_\mathcal{D}\bigr)^{-1} r_\text{red}(z) \] \[ z^{n+1} \;=\; z^n + \Delta z \]

Iterate From cold start $z = 0$. Converges in $\le 8$ iterations.

Latent z evolves · decoded ũ(z) approaches target u* · $\|r_\text{red}\|$ drops geometrically.

3. Inside one Gauss-Newton iteration

Initial guess Cold start in latent space: \[ z^{(0)} = 0 \;\in\; \mathbb{R}^k \]

Decode Lift the embedding to the full mesh: \[ \tilde{u}^{(0)} = m \odot \mathcal{D}(z^{(0)}) + u_g \]

FOM residual Evaluate the discretised PDE on the lifted state: \[ R^{(0)} = K\tilde{u}^{(0)} - F \;\in\; \mathbb{R}^N \]

Jacobian Manifold tangent (matrix-free, JAX VJP): \[ J_\mathcal{D} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{D}}{\partial z}\bigg|_{z^{(0)}} \;\in\; \mathbb{R}^{N\times k} \]

Galerkin project Contract residual with Jacobian — collapse $\mathbb{R}^N \to \mathbb{R}^k$: \[ r_\text{red}^{(0)} = J_\mathcal{D}^{\top}\, R^{(0)} \;\in\; \mathbb{R}^k \]

Solve Reduced Hessian, $k\!\times\!k$ linear solve: \[ H\,\Delta z = -r_\text{red}^{(0)}, \quad H = J_\mathcal{D}^{\top} K J_\mathcal{D} \]

Update embedding New latent state, ready for the next iteration: \[ z^{(1)} = z^{(0)} + \Delta z \]

4. Galerkin Projection — Heat (parabolic)

Semi-discrete FEM in space gives an ODE in time, with mass matrix $M$: \[ M\,\frac{du}{dt} + Ku \;=\; F \]

Project Substitute $\tilde{u}(z)$ and project by $J_\mathcal{D}^\top$: \[ J_\mathcal{D}^\top M J_\mathcal{D}\,\frac{dz}{dt} \;=\; J_\mathcal{D}^\top \bigl(F - K\tilde{u}(z)\bigr) \]

Backward Euler Each implicit time step is a $k \times k$ linear solve: \[ \bigl(J_\mathcal{D}^\top M J_\mathcal{D} + \Delta t\, J_\mathcal{D}^\top K J_\mathcal{D}\bigr)\, z_{n+1} \;=\; J_\mathcal{D}^\top M J_\mathcal{D}\, z_n + \Delta t\, J_\mathcal{D}^\top F \]

March in time CG in $\mathbb{R}^k$, warm-started from $z_n$. Heat diffuses, latent state evolves smoothly.

Initial bump diffuses; high-frequency latent modes decay first (physical).

5. Empirical Quadrature — pick the 1% that matter

The cost problem Even after projecting to $k$ dims, the projected residual is a sum over all $N$ mesh nodes: \[ r_\text{red}(z) \;=\; J_\mathcal{D}^\top R \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{N} J_{i,:}^\top R_i \] Cost is $\mathcal{O}(N)$ per Gauss-Newton step. We need to break that.

The EQ trick Replace the full sum with a weighted sum over a sparse subset $\mathcal{S}\subset\{1,\dots,N\}$: \[ r_\text{red}(z) \;\approx\; \sum_{i\in\mathcal{S}} w_i\, J_{i,:}^\top R_i \] with $|\mathcal{S}| = N_\text{eq} \ll N$ (typically $\sim$ 0.3% of mesh).

How we pick S Solve a non-negative least-squares problem on training snapshots: \[ \min_{w \geq 0}\; \|G^\top w - b\|_2 \] NNLS naturally produces a sparse $w$ — most weights are exactly zero, and the non-zero indices give us $\mathcal{S}$.

All mesh nodes (blue) → NNLS-selected subset (orange). Online cost no longer scales with $N$.

Like polling. Ask the 1% of nodes NNLS calls most informative, not all $N$. Weights $w$ reproduce the full sum on training snapshots. For Heat, EQ isn't optional — without it the operator is ill-conditioned and the solver diverges.

6. Matrix-free via JAX — never form J

The problem The decoder Jacobian $J_\mathcal{D} \in \mathbb{R}^{N\times k}$ is enormous: \[ N = 10^6,\; k = 24 \;\Longrightarrow\; J\;\text{has}\;\sim\!2.5\times 10^7\;\text{entries} \approx 96~\text{MB} \] Times 8 GN iters, 50 timesteps, batch … OOM on any GPU.

JAX gives us J·v for free Forward-mode AD (JVP) — one decoder forward pass: \[ J_\mathcal{D}\, v \;=\; \texttt{jax.jvp}(\mathcal{D},\, z,\, v),\quad v \in \mathbb{R}^k \] Cost: same as one decoder eval. Output: $\mathbb{R}^N$ vector.

… and Jᵀ·w too Reverse-mode AD (VJP) — one backward pass: \[ J_\mathcal{D}^\top\, w \;=\; \texttt{jax.vjp}(\mathcal{D},\, z)(w),\quad w \in \mathbb{R}^N \] Cost: same as one decoder eval. Output: $\mathbb{R}^k$ vector.

The Hessian-vector product The reduced Hessian action $H\,p = J^\top K J\, p$ is three operations: \[ p \xrightarrow{\text{JVP}} J p \xrightarrow{K} K J p \xrightarrow{\text{VJP}} J^\top K J p \] CG inner solver only ever needs $H\!\cdot\!p$. We never form $H$ either.

The trick: JAX never asks "what is J?" — it asks "what does J do to this vector?". Same answer, no memory.
